## Summary

The search for a unified theory of quantum gravity remains one of physics' most fundamental challenges. The Tensor-Driven Inertia (TDI) framework approaches this problem by treating inertia not as a fundamental property, but as an emergent phenomenon arising from the interaction between quantum fields and spacetime geometry.

Rather than quantizing gravity or geometrizing quantum mechanics, TDI proposes a third path: both gravitational and quantum effects emerge from tensor field dynamics in a unified geometric setting.

## Key Findings

1. **Emergent Inertia**: Inertial mass arises naturally from the coupling between matter fields and spacetime curvature tensors, without requiring the Higgs mechanism as the sole source of mass.
2. **Scale Bridging**: The framework smoothly interpolates between quantum and classical regimes, with the transition governed by a single geometric parameter.
3. **Formal Verification**: All core equations have been verified using the Lean 4 proof assistant, ensuring mathematical consistency of the derivation chain from axioms to predictions.

## Methodology

The derivation begins from a minimal set of geometric axioms about tensor field behavior in curved spacetime. Using variational principles and differential geometry, we derive field equations that reduce to Einstein's equations in the classical limit and reproduce standard quantum mechanical predictions at small scales. Each step has been formally verified for mathematical correctness.

## Implications

If validated empirically, TDI would provide a mathematically rigorous bridge between general relativity and quantum mechanics — addressing the hierarchy problem, dark energy, and the cosmological constant problem within a single consistent framework.
