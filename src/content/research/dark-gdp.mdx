## Summary

When you scroll a social media feed, watch algorithmically recommended content, or interact with any digital interface that monitors your behavior, you are not merely consuming a free product. You are simultaneously *producing* a distinct economic output — behavioral data that serves as a critical input to AI training, targeted advertising, and algorithmic product development. This production is not compensated by wages. It is not recorded in any national accounting system.

**Dark GDP** quantifies this hidden output: the aggregate shadow value of unpriced behavioral data labor, invisible to the System of National Accounts.

## Key Findings

1. **Magnitude**: 5.5 billion connected people generate approximately 13 trillion hours of Entangled Time per year — roughly 4× the total hours of the formal global labor force.
2. **Conservative Estimate**: Using only directly observable revenue channels (digital advertising, enterprise AI, data brokerage), Dark GDP is approximately $1.3 trillion/year — about 1.2% of global GDP.
3. **Adjusted Estimate**: Including unmeasured channels (foundation model training, implicit RLHF, background data collection, biometric bandwidth), the adjusted Dark GDP is $10–15 trillion/year, recovering the majority of the observed labor share decline.
4. **Zero-Wage Equilibrium**: The profit-maximizing fiat wage for data labor is exactly zero — a market outcome arising from perfectly inelastic labor supply, not from moral failure.

## Methodology

The framework extends Becker's (1965) time allocation model by introducing a third time state — Simultaneous Production-Consumption — in which the agent's utility and the firm's data capital are both increasing. Existence is established via Brouwer's Fixed Point Theorem, uniqueness via the Banach Contraction Mapping Theorem, and welfare characterization via constrained Pareto analysis. Empirical identification is proposed via Apple's App Tracking Transparency (ATT) as a natural experiment, using a 2SLS estimator.

## Implications

This work has direct implications for antitrust policy (proposing an Algorithmic Monopsony Standard to replace the Consumer Welfare Standard), tax design (an Algorithmic Severance Tax on extracted data capital), and human capital policy (a Cognitive Depreciation Allowance funded by severance tax revenue). The model proves these interventions are effective only during the transition — the window for policy action is closing.
